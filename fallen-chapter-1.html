CHAPTER 1

The Name I Never Forgot

The first thing I learned about photography was this: People show you what they want you to see. Everything else lives in the details they don't notice.

"Closer," I say. "Just a little."

The couple adjusts on the bench. The woman leans into him, her hand resting lightly over her stomach; protective, almost instinctive. He follows without hesitation, drawing her in like it's second nature.

I lift the camera. The light is soft, late afternoon gold, settling gently along their shoulders. It makes everything look warmer than it is. Safer.

"Perfect." I press the shutter. Once. Twice. Enough to freeze the moment before it shifts. They relax immediately after, like holding still was the hardest part.

"How do they look?" she asks.

I glance at the screen, scrolling through the frames. "They look like something you'll want to keep," I say. It's the kind of answer people expect.

She smiles anyway, satisfied. "Thank you, Zara."

I nod, already capping the lens. "Of course."

I've learned people don't really want honesty from photographs. Not the tension in their shoulders. Not the uncertainty behind their eyes. They want something clean. Something complete. So, I give them that. I pack up without looking back. The tripod collapses with a soft click. The camera strap settles against my shoulder. Behind me, they're laughing about something small, something I don't need to hear. I leave before it can mean anything.

The apartment is quiet when I get home. Not peaceful. Just quiet. I drop my keys into the bowl by the door and set my camera on the table, the weight of it familiar against my palm. Routine fills the space easily... coat off, shoes by the wall, a glass of water I won't finish. The silence settles in like it belongs here. I flip through the photos again. They're good. Balanced. Soft in all the right places. My thumb pauses on one frame. Her hand over her stomach. I hold there a second too long. Then I swipe past it. The screen goes dark.

My phone buzzes against the counter. Ravenna. I let it ring once before answering.

"Please tell me you're not busy tonight," she says.

"I just got back from work."

"Great. Then you're free."

I lean against the counter, closing my eyes briefly. "What is it?"

"Nothing dramatic," she says, which means the opposite. "Just people from the group. Drinks. Catching up."

I almost say no. Then, "Adrian's back."

The words land clean. No warning. No space to prepare. I open my eyes.

"I know," I say.

A pause. "You knew?" she asks.

"Someone mentioned it." His name has a way of finding its way back, even when I don't go looking.

"He's only here for a few weeks," Ravenna says. "We're doing a welcome thing tonight."

My grip tightens slightly around the glass. "Ravenna..."

"Before you say no, just think about it."

"I don't need to think about it."

"Zara."

I don't respond.

"You can't keep avoiding him forever."

"I'm not avoiding him." The lie is thin.

"Right," she says. "You've just successfully not been in the same room for four years."

I let that sit.

"Just come," she says, softer now. "You don't have to stay long. Say hi. Be civil."

Civil. Like there's a version of this that's easy.

"I'll think about it," I say.

"Thinking isn't coming."

"I know."

A pause. "He might bring someone," she adds.

Something in my chest tightens before I can stop it. "Okay," I say. But it doesn't sound like anything.

We hang up. The apartment is quiet again. I set my phone down and stare at it for a moment, like it might explain something I'm not ready to understand. Adrian's back. The thought doesn't settle. It just... lingers. I push away from the counter and move into the living room. My gaze drifts to the bottom shelf of the bookcase. There's a box there. I haven't opened it in years. I don't open it now. But I don't look away, either. For a moment, I consider it. Just to see. Just to remember. I turn away instead. Some things don't need revisiting to still matter. I pick up my journal from the table and flip to the last page. The question is still there. What are you really afraid of? My pen hovers over the paper. The answer presses forward before I can stop it.

I'm afraid of seeing him again. I'm afraid nothing has changed. I'm afraid everything has.

I close the journal without writing it down. Across the room, my phone lights up again. A message from Ravenna. He might be bringing someone. The words settle heavier this time. Of course he is. Four years is enough time for anything to change. I picture it without meaning to. Adrian walking into that room. Laughing. Easy. Unaffected. Standing next to someone who fits into his life the way I used to. The thought lands somewhere deep and quiet. Not sharp enough to be anger. Not distant enough to ignore. I exhale slowly and reach for my coat. Maybe Ravenna's right. Maybe I should go. Not because I want to. But because I need to know. If seeing him again will finally feel like nothing... or remind me of everything. I grab my keys. Pause at the door. For a second, I almost stay. But I don't. Because some questions don't disappear just because you refuse to answer them. And tonight, I think I'm done pretending I don't have any.
